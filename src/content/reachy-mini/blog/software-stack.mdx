## Introduction

[Reachy Mini](https://huggingface.co/blog/reachy-mini) is a small open-source
robot: a head that moves, two antennas, a microphone, a speaker and a camera.
It fits on a desk. You can talk to it, it can look at you, and it can react.

The robot is only half the story. The other half is the galaxy around it: a smartphone
app to set it up and talk to it, a store full of things it can do, a couple of
small desktop tools, and the Hugging Face ecosystem to back it all. None of it
asks you to know robotics. This article is a tour of those pieces, one by one,
in the order you would meet them as a new owner.

## Using your robot

### The mobile app

The main way to use Reachy Mini is the mobile app. It is what sets up a
brand-new robot in the first place - finding it over Bluetooth and putting it
on your Wi-Fi - and once the robot is online, you sign in with your Hugging
Face account, your robot shows up in a list, and you tap to connect.
Everything you do day to day starts here.

<Note variant="info" emoji="🗣️" title="Conversation is built in">
The headline feature is talking to the robot. The conversation actually runs on
the robot itself; the app is simply how you start it and follow along. Start it
and you are in a real back-and-forth voice conversation: you speak, the robot
listens, thinks, and answers out loud while its head and antennas move along. It
can also glance through its camera, play an emotion, and remember things you
told it earlier.
</Note>

<Video src="/videos/conversation.mp4" aspect="1004 / 1730" maxWidth={320}>Talking to Reachy Mini from the mobile app: hold a real voice conversation, and give it any personality you like from a single line.</Video>

<Stack layout="3-column" gap="medium">
  <Note variant="neutral" emoji="✍️" title="Describe a vibe">
  One sentence is enough. Out comes a name, a voice and a whole character.
  </Note>
  <Note variant="neutral" emoji="🎲" title="Or roll the dice">
  No idea? Hit surprise-me and the app invents an original character for you.
  </Note>
  <Note variant="neutral" emoji="💾" title="Keep your cast">
  A few personas ship built in; the ones you make are saved and swappable.
  </Note>

<Sidenote>
Personalities are generated with your own Hugging Face account, so there is no
shared key behind the scenes and nothing extra to set up. Sign in and create.
</Sidenote>

### Drive it, and add to it

Beyond conversation, the app lets you watch the camera feed and steer the head
with an on-screen joystick, like a small remote-controlled companion. And it is
your door to the app store: browse new apps and launch them straight onto the
robot, no install (more on that later).

The whole app works whether the robot is right in front of you or in another
room, another building, or another country. If you can reach the internet, you
can reach your robot.

### Lite or full: two robots, one ecosystem

There are two versions of the hardware, aimed at two kinds of people.

<Stack layout="2-column" gap="large">
  <Note variant="neutral" emoji="🤖" title="Reachy Mini (wireless)">
  The polished, end-user experience. Its computer is inside, it lives on your
  Wi-Fi, and everything in this article - setup, conversation, the store -
  happens from the phone in your hand.
  </Note>
  <Note variant="neutral" emoji="🔧" title="Reachy Mini lite">
  The version for makers and tinkerers. Same head, antennas, camera and
  expressiveness, but no computer inside: it plugs into yours over USB, and
  your machine does the thinking.
  </Note>
</Stack>

The lite needs one extra piece: the **desktop app**, which runs the robot's
software on your computer. With it, a lite robot joins the exact same
ecosystem as a wireless one - the same mobile app, the same store, the same
apps.

### The flasher: a reset button for the wireless robot

Sometimes software gets into a bad state. When every other recovery has
failed, a small desktop app - the **flasher** - puts the wireless Reachy Mini
back to factory fresh. Plug the robot in over USB, click one button, and it
reinstalls the robot's whole operating system, downloading the latest version
for you.

<Sidenote>
The flasher is designed for the full version: it is the recovery tool you reach for
when a robot will not boot, not something you need in normal use.
</Sidenote>

<Video src="/videos/flasher-demo.mp4" aspect="1000 / 944" maxWidth={560}>The flasher: plug the robot in over USB, click once, and it reinstalls a fresh operating system, downloading the latest version for you.</Video>

## The app store and the host

### One store, open to everyone

Reachy Mini has an app store, and you reach it right from the mobile app.
Browse the list, tap an app, and it runs on your robot. There is nothing to
install and nothing to update.

Every app in the store is a web page that anyone can publish. Post yours and it
can show up in the store like any other. Publishing is open to everyone.

<Note variant="info" emoji="🛡️" title="Open, but not a free-for-all">
A featured list highlights trusted apps, a blocklist removes bad ones, and an
automatic filter screens content before it reaches users. Publishing stays
easy; the store stays safe to browse.
</Note>

### Two ways to run any app

The same app runs in two places, with no change and no second version:

<Stack layout="2-column" gap="large">
  <Note variant="neutral" emoji="📱" title="Inside the mobile app">
  Open an app from the store and it runs right there in the app, handed your
  connected robot automatically. Close it and you are back to the conversation.
  </Note>
  <Note variant="neutral" emoji="🌐" title="From the host, in any browser">
  No mobile app at hand? The <strong>host</strong> is a web page that turns
  any browser into a full Reachy Mini client. Open an app link and it runs there.
  </Note>
</Stack>

Either way you sign in and pick your robot once, and from then on every app you
open is already connected. No separate login per app, no reconnecting. An app
author ships only the app itself; the mobile app and the host handle the rest.

Because the host is just a web page, sharing an app is like sharing any link.
Whoever opens it signs in with their own account and uses their own robot.
</Sidenote>

### Near or far, the same app

Whether your robot is on the same Wi-Fi as you or on the other side of the
world, the app connects the same way and behaves the same way. The connection
(WebRTC, if you want the name) finds the best path on its own and carries the
video, the sound and the movement together.

<Note variant="success" emoji="🌍" title="One app, local or remote">
The app never has to know where the robot is. Drive one on your desk over local
Wi-Fi, or one in another country over the internet, with the exact same app and
the exact same tap.
</Note>

## Make your own, in plain words

You should be able to describe what you want and watch it happen on your
robot. The tool for that is an **AI agent**, and there are three ways to put
one to work.

### Describe an app, watch an agent build it

The [**Reachy Mini vibe coder**](https://huggingface.co/spaces/tfrere/reachy-mini-vibe-coding-apps)
is a coding agent that writes small robot apps with you. The word *agent*
matters: it does not just paste a snippet. It plans the app, writes the actual
files, runs them on your robot, watches what breaks, and fixes it, looping the
way a developer would until the thing works.

<Note variant="info" emoji="🤖" title="It knows about the robot">
The agent is briefed on how Reachy Mini works: how the head moves, what is
safe, which SDK commands to call. The app it produces respects the hardware,
and you never have to explain robotics to it.
</Note>

You stay in the loop the whole time: you describe, it builds, you watch it run
live on your real robot, and you ask for changes in plain words.

  Ask for what you want, in a sentence.
  </Note>
  <Note variant="neutral" emoji="🔁" title="Let it work">
  The agent writes, runs and fixes the code on its own.
  </Note>
  <Note variant="neutral" emoji="👀" title="Preview">
  It runs live on your actual robot.
  </Note>

### What the agent actually produces

A Reachy Mini app is a web page. Not a firmware image, not a robotics
project - a web page that drives the robot through a small JavaScript library:

```js
const robot = new ReachyMini();
await robot.autoConnect(); // sign in, find the robot, connect, wake it up
await robot.moveHead({ pitch: 0.1, yaw: -0.2 }); // tilt and turn the head
await robot.playMove("happy");                   // play a ready-made emotion
```

That is all the agent is writing for you - and if you can build a web page,
you can also write one by hand whenever you want more control than a prompt
gives you. An app can even lean on the robot's built-in conversation instead
of reinventing it: start it, make the robot speak, or hand it a whole
personality, each one command away.

<Sidenote>
What the SDK does under these calls - the connection, the sessions, the
safety machinery that always returns the robot to a safe pose - is the
subject of the technical companion article.
</Sidenote>

### Or bring your own agent

Already using a coding agent like Claude Code, Codex or Copilot? Point it at
the project's [AGENTS.md guide](https://github.com/pollen-robotics/reachy_mini/blob/main/AGENTS.md)
and describe your idea. The guide hands your agent the same robot knowledge
and the same patterns the vibe coder relies on. Same destination, your own
toolbox.

<Sidenote>
The hosted vibe coder is the fastest path; your own agent gives you the most
control. Both produce ordinary apps that live in the same store.
</Sidenote>

### Or just change its personality

You do not even need to make an app. The persona creator from earlier is the
same idea aimed at the robot's character instead of its code: one sentence in,
a whole personality out, no code at all.

Describe it in words, and something appears that drives the robot. Whether it
is a full app or a new personality, the path from idea to a moving, talking
robot is short.

## Wrapping up

Reachy Mini is a small robot, but the software around it is what makes it feel
open. You set it up with an app, you add to it from a store, and when you want
something that does not exist yet, you describe it and an agent builds it.

None of this asks you to be a roboticist. The app hides the setup, the SDK
hides the robotics, and the store hides the deployment. What is left is
deciding what your robot should do.

<Note variant="info" emoji="🤖" title="The takeaway">
An open robot is only as open as the software around it. Reachy Mini's software
is built so that using it, and building for it, are things anyone can do.
</Note>

If you have a Reachy Mini, or just an idea for what one should do, the shortest
path from here is to describe it and watch it move.
